Sending the same generic resume to every job is one of the biggest reasons applications get ignored. Recruiters and ATS tools both look for **relevance** — how closely your experience matches what the role requires.

## Why tailoring matters

A job description is essentially a wish list. When your resume mirrors that list with real examples from your background, you signal:

- You read the role carefully
- You have the required skills
- You are worth interviewing

Studies suggest tailored resumes can receive **2–3× more callbacks** than generic ones.

## Step 1: Highlight must-have requirements

Print or copy the job description. Mark:

- **Required skills** (e.g. Python, SQL, client communication)
- **Preferred skills** (nice to have)
- **Responsibilities** (what you will do day to day)
- **Industry terms** (e.g. B2B SaaS, fintech, healthcare)

These become your keyword target list.

## Step 2: Rewrite your summary

Your summary (3–4 lines) should name the role you are targeting and hit 3–5 top keywords.

**Example for a Digital Marketing role:**

> Digital marketing specialist with 4+ years in performance campaigns, Google Ads, and SEO. Drove 40% YoY lead growth for B2B SaaS products. Experienced in analytics, A/B testing, and cross-channel reporting.

## Step 3: Reorder and rewrite experience bullets

Put the most relevant job first in your bullet list for each role. Rewrite bullets to reflect the language in the posting:

| Job description says | Your bullet could say |
|---|---|
| "Manage stakeholder relationships" | "Managed stakeholder relationships across product, sales, and engineering for 12 enterprise accounts" |
| "Data-driven decision making" | "Used SQL and Looker dashboards to inform pricing decisions, increasing margin by 8%" |

## Step 4: Add a skills section aligned to the role

Group skills the way the employer groups them. If the JD mentions "Technical: Java, Spring Boot" and "Soft skills: leadership," mirror that structure.

## How much tailoring is enough?

You do not need to rewrite everything from scratch each time. For each application:

- Update the **summary**
- Adjust **5–8 bullets** across your recent roles
- Refresh the **skills** section
- Match the **job title** in your headline if accurate

## Red flags to avoid

- Copy-pasting the job description verbatim (keyword stuffing)
- Claiming skills you cannot demonstrate in an interview
- Changing your job title to something you never held

Tailoring is about **honest alignment**, not exaggeration.
